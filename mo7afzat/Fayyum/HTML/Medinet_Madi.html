Medinet Madi



The ancient city of Madi in Fayoum Governorate is one of the most important archaeological monuments that were discovered in Fayoum Governorate. Its importance is due to the fact that it is the oldest temple of the Middle Kingdom that has been discovered in Egypt so far, in addition to having the longest archaeological road in Egypt, which made it put Fayoum on the map of the governorates that have antiquities. It is an important destination for many visitors, experts and archaeologists around the world.

Sayed Al-Shoura, Director General of Fayoum Antiquities, said in exclusive statements to "The Seventh Day", that the ancient city of Madi is located 35 kilometers west of Fayoum Governorate, near Ezbet Al-Kashef, south of Bahr Al-Banat, a city that includes the ruins of temples built by Amenemhat III and IV of the family. The twelfth century, and some frills were added to it in the Roman era, after statues of lions with human heads were placed, and it is considered the largest surviving temple of the Middle Kingdom in Egypt

Al-Shura pointed out that this temple was discovered in 1937, when the Milan University mission was working in the area, noting that the design of the temple is very simple, consistent with the general feature of the layout of the temples of the Middle Kingdom, and its axis is straight and goes from north to south, and the temple consists of an attribute topped by two columns with crowns. Postage representing the postal bundle, and it carried the ceiling and adorned the facade of the temple, an Egyptian corniche. From the adjective, we reach an entrance that leads to a transverse hall that ends with three chapels, the largest of which is the middle compartment, in which was found a one-piece statue of the harvest goddess Ranoun, in the middle of the two kings Amenemhat III and Amenemhat IV. And in front of them all was a sacrifice table whose place on the ground could be determined.

It is noticeable that the inscriptions on the western side all bear the name of King Amenemhat III, while those on the eastern side bear the name of King Amenemhat IV. The left compartment was decorated with scenes of offering offerings to the Sobek instrument, and in front of him on the opposite side the king presents a perfume vase to the gods Rannout in the form of a cobra snake .

From the remains of the scenes and texts found in this temple, it is clear that some of them represent one of the stages of the temple’s foundation rituals, which is the tug-of-war ritual, and some refer to the name of the first hall as the Transfiguration Hall. Rennout and the goddess Isis, and scenes of the purification ceremonies.

Sayed Al-Shura pointed out that the temple was dedicated to the two gods, the crocodile “Sobek” and the snake “snake” Rannott. The city includes the Rams Road, the longest archaeological road in Egypt to be discovered. It was designed during the reign of King Amenemhat III in the Twelfth Dynasty and completed by his son Amenemhat IV.

Al-Shura confirmed that the mission of the Italian University of Pisa made a discovery through rams, 336 meters long and 7 meters wide, and includes 23 statues, including 5 unique statues of lions and a female lion standing, feeding lions from both sides of each statue, an altar for offering offerings, several chapels and paintings engraved with gifts from those who He established the road of rams in the city, called "Protar Kos, son of Rhodes", his wife "Famista" and his sons, all of whom give the road, the booths and statues to Queen Cleopatra and King Ptolemy as a gift from them in appreciation of their love for their mother. Among the most important monuments of the city is the limestone temple of the Middle Kingdom and a procession road bordered on both sides by two rows of sphinxes.

Abdel-Al adds that the Rams Road is considered the longest road for rams in Egypt, and there are plaques engraved on the road with a dedication to the builder of the road, his wife and his sons. Sobek, the god of Fayoum.
